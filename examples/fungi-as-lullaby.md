**Topic:** fungi
**Exercise:** Found Poem — terminology from mycology turned into liturgy
**Constraint:** as a lullaby (sung shape, soft consonants where possible, must rock)
**Reflection:** Inventory of Strangeness

---

*Hyphae, Hyphae*

Hush now, hyphae,
thread the dark loam,
braid through the rootlets,
find your way home.

Sleep, little mycelium,
under the leaves,
the alder is listening,
the alder believes.

Soft, little fruiting body —
push up, push up —
the rain has a saucer,
the moss has a cup.

Hush now, hyphae,
the forest is fed.
Everything dying
is somebody's bread.

---

**Inventory of Strangeness:**

- *hyphae* is already half a lullaby word — two soft breaths, no hard stops
- mycelial networks are sometimes called the "wood-wide web" but the better phrase is the older one: *the common mycorrhizal network*
- fruiting bodies are not the organism; they're its messages
- decomposers don't destroy — they relocate
- the closing rhyme arrived before the meaning did, which is how lullabies are supposed to work
